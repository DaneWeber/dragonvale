# Murder Investigation at Barret Poyn's

## Introduction

King's Keep, the seat of the bustling canton of Reichsfreyt in the heart of Hallstadt, is no stranger to the presence of Aidar, Jovrim, and Verdigriss. After they and their allies exposed and defeated Overlord Ptang Mowbrey, these heroes have been welcomed and respected. They have also made a habit of visiting and tending to suspicious and criminal activities in the canton as no new Lord Reichsfreyt has been chosen. The consulate was unsure whether they were ready to trust another individual with the most powerful lordship in the valley. There have been different proposals, including granting the title to one of the heroes, but thus far none had been chosen.

The Lords meet with the mayor of King's Keep, the honorable Chim Bowman. While reviewing the petty crimes and regular ruckuses caused by travelers, a clerk steps in and relates that a messenger boy has just brought the news that a murder has been committed in the temple district. All murders must be investigated by the mayor, acting as Lord Protector, but Chim, a heavy-set man praised for his management and little else, requests that the visiting Lords join him. As is their habit, they do. While not in full battle regalia, the three heros are armed and prepared for the sorts of surprises that sometimes accompany criminal activity.

The men arrive at the scene of the crime, a furniture shop near the east gate. A brightly painted unicorn, invoking the patronage of Ehlonna, decorates the door, which is flanked with several chairs under the shop's overhang. There is little traffic on the street and a sole guard stands at the door. Within the store are several more pieces of wood furniture and an open wood-working shop. There are two internal doors leading out of the main shop area, as well as a door clearly leading out to the back. Sitting on his own merchandise is the carpenter. He is splattered in blood on his face, hands, and much of his leather apron.

The sheriff explained in hushed tones that the victim's corpse is still in the basement. The story is that a city guard was walking nearby when he heard a horrific shriek, ran into the shop to investigate, called out, and met the carpenter running up the stairs from the basement.

"It falls to me to bring the murder charges, you know," Chim states to the Lords, "but I still cannot abide this work. It makes me sick. Since I have the three of you, can I beg you to investigate?"

After a pause, Verdigriss answers Chim in the unnatural, rocks-scraping-together voice that only Jovrim and Aidar know is actually the mask's voice. "Yessss." Verdigriss then descends into the basement to investigate the crime scene, leaving Aidar to do the interrogation, as is their habit.

In the basement Verdigriss examines the murder scene. The wall opposite the street has large double doors. The basement has shelving along three walls. Or had. The shelving along the wall opposite the stairs is broken up on the floor. In fact, the entire floor is covered in wreckage. Splintered wood, much discolored with what he presumed to be blood, is strewn about. There is a small lamp tipped over several feet from the base of the stairs. In fact, if the wizard hadn't been so slow to enter the room, he would have stepped on . . . something. It's hard to make out, and Verdigriss' knowledge of anatomy is poor, but some combination of gore and blood and clothing rests immediately at the foot of the stairs.

Aidar walks over to where the carpenter sits, pauses for a moment considering the blood and and the look on the man's face, then leans forward slightly, which has the disconcerting effect of emphasizing the difference in height rather than bringing his face any closer. His dark eyes are empty of any threat or anger, and his voice calm and firm, but both are also lacking any hint of compassion. "I assume that you wish to give us an explanation of what has happened here?"

The carpenter looks up from his hands, and recognition and relief plays across his face. He leaps up, attempting to grab Aidar, who deftly twists away, as if he had been expecting this. "Lord Aidar! It's me! Thank the gods you've come! This is horrible. Just horrible." The carpenter is clearly excited, standing with outstretched bloody arms looking to hug Aidar.

Jovrim reveals no hint of surprise at this turn of events. He sits quietly in the corner and continues watching the "interrogation" intently, paying special attention to the reactions of the Chim. Aidar, on the other hand, gives the same response he typically gives the unexpected: the threat of swift and terrible violence. He sword is out of it's sheath faster than anything that big has a right to be, and the room is washed with a burst of cold as the blade hits the air. Aidar does not point the sword at the blood-splattered carpenter, but holds the blade across his own body, defensively. "Do I know you?" he asks.

The carpenter shivers with both a literal and figurative chill. ". . . yes," he eeks out. "Um, three years ago when I was moving to the valley, my caravan was raided by the beastman demons from the woods. You and your friends appeared out of Celestia and saved us. You accompanied us the rest of the way here to King's Keep. You were all so generous and turned down the money we collected to thank you. We even talked over those two days on the road. You don't remember me? I'm Barret Poyn. I certainly haven't forgetten any of you."

While Aidar and friends remember rescuing several caravans from gnoll raiding parties, and they certainly would turn down the minuscule monetary offerings that the poor peasant immigrants would scrape together, Barret does not stick out. His story is certainly believable, however, and Aidar certainly couldn't swear to him not having been among them.

Chim and the others were startled by the unsheathing of the sword and grip their weapons. Tension fills the room.

Javrim remains seated and slowly turns his gaze from Chim to Aidar. "Aidar, put your sword away," he states with just a hint of admonishment. Javrim slowly turns his head toward the carpenter, and, for the first time since he arrived, focuses his complete attention on the Barret. "It is immaterial to your situation whether we saved you in the past or not. Please, return to the matter at hand. Tell us what happened here today."

Aidar's eyes flick momentarily to Jarvim, then he makes an almost imperceptible nod. Keeping his distance from Barret, Aidar makes one more warning gesture with the sword, then returns it to its sheath. With only a hint of wounded pride, he crosses his arms over his chest, and cocks an eyebrow. "Yes, I am sure I am not the only one *dying* to know what happened."

"Well, I suppose you're far too important to remember me," a note of disappointment tinges Barret's voice. "I was just so happy that you came to clear this all up. These guards have been keeping me company, but they haven't done much to figure out what was in my basement! I heard some sort of commotion this morning and lit a shallow lamp to look into the basement. Walking down the stairs is the last thing I remember. I woke up in the dark. When I tried to get up, my hands were deep in entrails and such. I screamed in horror. After getting my bearings, I ran upstairs and thankfully this guard was walking into my shop. He said he would send for help, but that I should just sit here." After a pause, Barret adds quietly, "It was horrific."

Worried about further outbursts from Aidar, Jovrim rises and slowly steps closer to Barret. In an attempt to connect with the carpenter, he awkwardly forces "I am sorry to hear about your ordeal, but we do need more answers." Jovrim silently circles the man, inspecting his body for bruises. "How did you feel when you first awoke? Were you sore? Or perhaps your body felt fine but your mind felt...not quite right?"

"I . . . wait, what? What are you getting at? I mean, I had a horrible headache when I awoke and I've certainly been feeling . . shocked. I can't believe that I had a handful of . . ." the carpenter spaces out for a second. "But if you have steely minds and iron stomachs, go see for yourself. I barely saw anything in the dark."

Barret looks down at his right arm. Following his gaze, Jovrim can see a tear in his shirt and a minor cut that has stopped bleeding. "Huh. I guess I did get hurt. I mean, my head hurts the most. But I don't remember this cut . . ." he trails off.

"Perhaps I *will* go see the victim for myself." Jovrim responds thoughtfully. He starts toward the stairs, but stops in stride and turns his gaze back toward his companion. "Aidar, try not to slice him up too much while I'm gone," he pleads, "he very well might be telling the truth." With that, he turns and descends into the basement.

Aidar glares at Jarvim's retreating back until it disappears down the stairs, then approaches Barret again, and continues the questioning. "So, before today, had anything new or unusual happened to you in the last few months? Strange commissions for unusual jobs? Threats from shadowy figures? Injuries you don't remember receiving? Waking up naked in the forest with a dead deer? Anything like that?"

"What? What!?! That's so . . . no. Nothing like that. Everything has been perfectly fine. I mean, it's hard starting up a new business and working out suppliers and everything." After Barret's shocked response, he glances over at Chim and the others and back to Aidar. "Apparently this old coffin-shop isn't the best location. But no, nothing like that."

Aidar's eyebrow twitches upward. He has caught a wiff of something he does not like. Although a man accustomed to death, violence, and abominations, he retained that instinctive fear and hatred of undeath so often found in primitive people.

"How long ago did you decide that plying your trade in a coffin shop would be a good idea? And when you say "coffin shop," do you mean that they made coffins here? Did they ever do anything else with coffins here? Like bury people in them?" Barret starts to stammer, but before he can respond to the increasingly rapid stream of questions, Aidar cuts him off with a final question, "And who is 'they'?"

"They? Who? You mean the former owners? I have no idea! I saved my money and bought this from the mayor," Barret points at Chim, "a year ago. It was declared abandoned and all. It was a really good deal. It was all set up for wood-working. I did the right thing: I had a handmaid of the huntress come out and dedicate my shop and I painted over the stern sign that was left. I'm sure some of the coffins were used for burials. There were even two left behind that I used to heat this place last winter."

Aidar made a sound that could be interpreted as acknowledgment, thought for a second, then turned to the mayor.
"What happened to the previous owners of this shop?"
